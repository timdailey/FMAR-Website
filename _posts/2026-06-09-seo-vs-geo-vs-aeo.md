SEO, GEO, and AEO get used interchangeably in most marketing conversations. They shouldn't. The confusion is understandable: they share vocabulary, some tactics, and the industry hasn't settled on clean definitions. But they produce different outcomes, get measured differently, and fixing one doesn't automatically fix the others.

This post lays out the distinctions clearly, shows where the three overlap, and gives you a practical framework for deciding where to invest.

## The Short Version

SEO gets your pages to rank in search engines. [GEO]({{ '/what-is-generative-engine-optimization-geo/' | relative_url }}) gets your brand cited in AI-generated answers. AEO gets your content extracted and used by AI answer engines when they generate those answers.

SEO optimizes at the page level. GEO optimizes at the brand entity level. AEO optimizes at the content chunk level. All three can run simultaneously, but they rely on different inputs and produce different measurable outputs.

If you're only doing SEO right now, you have a gap. AI-driven research is already part of how buyers in most categories form shortlists, and SEO investment doesn't automatically translate into AI visibility.

## What Each One Optimizes For

Search engine optimization is about pages. You create content, earn authority, and compete for ranking positions in an index. Google retrieves pages that match queries and returns them ranked by relevance and authority. The feedback loop is between your content and a search index.

Generative engine optimization is about brand entities. AI answer engines don't return pages ranked by relevance. They generate answers by synthesizing information from multiple sources. Whether your brand gets named in those answers depends on whether AI systems have enough consistent, independently verified information about you to cite you with confidence. The feedback loop is between your brand's external signals and the entity recognition systems AI platforms use.

Answer engine optimization is about content chunks. When AI systems generate answers, they extract specific pieces of content: a direct answer to a question, a specific claim, a defined term. AEO is about structuring your content so those extraction events happen in your favor. The feedback loop is between your content's format and structure and the extraction patterns AI systems are trained on.

The cleanest way to see the distinction: SEO determines whether you're in the index. GEO determines whether AI systems know your brand. AEO determines whether AI systems can use your content when they do.

## The Signal Comparison

Each discipline relies on a distinct signal stack. The overlap is real but smaller than most teams assume.

| | SEO | GEO | AEO |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary unit** | Web page | Brand entity | Content chunk |
| **Goal** | Rank in search results | Get cited in AI answers | Get extracted by AI systems |
| **Core signals** | Backlinks, keyword relevance, page authority | Entity profiles, schema markup, third-party mentions | Direct-answer formatting, FAQPage schema, content structure |
| **Key platforms** | Google, Bing | ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Google AI Overviews | All AI answer engines |
| **Output** | Ranked search listing | Brand name in AI response | Content used in AI response |
| **Measurement** | Rankings, clicks, impressions | Citation rate, mention position, Share of AI Voice | Content extraction rate, AI Overviews inclusion |
| **Fastest wins** | On-page optimization, technical fixes | Schema markup, entity profile completion | Content restructuring, FAQPage schema |
| **Longest-lead wins** | Domain authority, backlink building | Wikipedia presence, press coverage | Topical authority, original research |
| **Managed by** | SEO team or agency | GEO specialist or AI visibility platform | Content team with GEO guidance |

Two signals work across all three: content quality and technical crawlability. A page that can't be crawled doesn't rank, doesn't get indexed for AI retrieval, and doesn't contribute to entity signals. Well-written, accurate content performs better across all three than thin or recycled material.

Everything else is more discipline-specific than it looks.

## Where They Overlap and Where They Don't

The overlap that's real: topical authority and content depth. Sites with comprehensive, interconnected coverage of a topic tend to perform better in all three channels than sites with isolated pages. Building a content cluster that covers a topic thoroughly serves SEO, helps GEO by signaling expertise, and creates more AEO opportunities by producing more extractable content.

FAQ content is a genuine crossover asset. Pages built as FAQ sections (with direct questions and immediate answers) are valuable for SEO (People Also Ask boxes, featured snippets), for GEO (content structure AI systems extract from), and for AEO (FAQPage schema makes Q&A pairs machine-readable). A well-built FAQ page with correct schema is probably the highest-value overlap investment available.

Here's where a lot of SEO teams hit a wall: backlinks don't transfer. They're the foundation of search authority, but inside an AI citation system, they're largely irrelevant. A brand with ten thousand backlinks and no G2 profile, no Wikipedia entry, and no Organization schema can rank well on Google and be completely absent from AI-generated answers. We see this regularly. Strong domain authority, zero AI visibility. Conversely, a brand with modest backlink authority but clean entity signals often outperforms much larger competitors in AI answers.

Keyword optimization also doesn't carry over the way teams expect. On-page keyword density, title tag optimization, and anchor text targeting are SEO mechanics with no direct equivalent in GEO. AI systems don't retrieve pages by matching keywords. They form confidence levels about entities and extract content based on structure and relevance. Optimizing for the same keywords you're targeting in SEO isn't a GEO strategy.

## Which One You Actually Need

The honest answer is that most marketing teams need all three running in parallel, but sequence and priority depend on where your gaps are.

**If your brand is invisible in AI-generated answers** across ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude, start with GEO. Specifically: entity signal completion (Google Business Profile, G2, Crunchbase, Wikidata) and Organization schema on your homepage. These are the fastest fixes and address the most fundamental gap. If AI systems can't recognize and verify your brand, neither better content structure nor stronger search rankings will fix it.

**If your brand appears in AI answers but isn't being cited in the specific queries that matter**, start with AEO. Audit your highest-value content pages for direct-answer structure, add FAQPage schema to your FAQ content, and restructure sections where the answer is buried behind introductory context. This addresses the content extraction layer without requiring new content or new profiles.

**If your brand has reasonable AI visibility but weak search traffic**, SEO investment makes sense. Organic search still drives significant buyer research volume, and some GEO improvements (content depth, topical authority) compound with SEO work.

**If you're starting from scratch**, the sequencing that produces the fastest results is: schema and entity profile fixes (days to weeks), content restructuring (weeks), new content creation (weeks to months), longer-lead entity building (months). This order reflects what shows measurable results fastest, not what's conceptually most important.

What the sequence isn't: doing SEO first and adding GEO later. Buyers who start their research in AI systems don't search Google afterward for the brands they didn't find. If you're not in the AI answer, you're not in their consideration set, regardless of what happens when they eventually do search.

Not sure where your gaps are? The [GEO audit framework]({{ '/geo-audit/' | relative_url }}) covers the full diagnostic: citation testing across platforms, entity signal review, schema audit, and content gap analysis.

## The Measurement Problem

We regularly talk to marketing teams that are trying to track their GEO and AEO performance using SEMrush or Ahrefs. It's a complete mismatch. Rank trackers show you where your pages appear in search results. They show you nothing about whether your brand is being named in a ChatGPT response or whether your content is being sourced in a Perplexity answer.

GEO requires different measurement entirely. Citation rate, citation position, and citation accuracy across a standard set of buyer-intent queries, tracked manually or with a dedicated tool across each AI platform you care about. [Share of AI Voice]({{ '/glossary/share-of-ai-voice/' | relative_url }}) (your citation rate as a percentage of total citations across your competitive set) is the competitive metric. None of this appears in a rank tracker.

AEO is measured through FAQPage schema eligibility in Google Search Console, manual testing of whether specific content is being sourced in [Google AI Overviews]({{ '/glossary/google-ai-overviews/' | relative_url }}), and checking whether content restructuring changes are showing up in extraction patterns over time.

The practical implication: if your organization measures marketing performance through a single dashboard that includes only SEO metrics, you have a blind spot. Brands investing in GEO and AEO won't appear in your rank tracking data even as they're capturing buyers before those buyers ever run a search.
