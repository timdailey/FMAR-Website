## The Marketing Playbook for Plastic Surgery Has a New Fifth Pillar

Marketing for plastic surgery practices used to come down to four channels: a strong website, active reputation management, paid media, and referral relationships. That playbook still works, but it's incomplete. A growing share of prospective patients now start their research by asking ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google's AI Overview a direct question, "who's the best plastic surgeon in [city]," "top-rated rhinoplasty near me," "board-certified surgeon for a mommy makeover." Those answers come from a different set of signals than a Google search results page, and most practices have never checked whether they show up in them.

This guide covers all five pillars: the four channels every practice marketing plan should already include, and the AI visibility work that's now sitting alongside them.

## Pillar One: Website and Local SEO

Your website remains the foundation. For a plastic surgery practice, that means procedure-specific landing pages (not just a single "services" page), before-and-after galleries with proper alt text and structured data, and location pages if you serve more than one city or have satellite offices.

Local SEO for a medical practice still depends on the fundamentals: a complete, verified Google Business Profile, consistent name, address, and phone number across every listing, and enough recent, high-quality reviews to signal an active, trusted practice. What's changed is that these same signals now double as inputs to how AI platforms evaluate your practice, which is covered in Pillar Five.

## Pillar Two: Reputation and Review Platforms

Plastic surgery is a reputation-driven category more than almost any other local business type. Prospective patients read reviews with far more scrutiny than they would for a restaurant or a home services company, because the decision involves their body and, often, a meaningful financial commitment.

Google reviews matter, but they're not the whole picture. RealSelf, where patients leave detailed reviews and "Worth It" ratings tied to specific procedures, and Vitals, which surfaces board-certification and credential data, are two platforms that carry outsized influence in this category specifically. Zwivel has grown quickly as a consultation and booking platform that some AI systems now reference directly. A practice that's only managing its Google reviews is leaving two of its three most-checked reputation sources unmanaged.

## Pillar Three: Paid Media

Paid search and paid social still drive a meaningful share of new-patient volume, particularly for high-consideration procedures where patients research for weeks or months before booking a consultation. The category has specific constraints worth knowing: several platforms restrict or review cosmetic and medical ad creative more closely than standard e-commerce ads, and before-and-after imagery often needs disclaimers or platform-specific approval.

Paid media works best as a complement to the other four pillars, not a replacement. A well-targeted ad campaign driving traffic to a practice with thin reputation signals or poor AI visibility will convert at a lower rate than the same spend driving traffic to a practice that shows up consistently everywhere a prospective patient looks.

## Pillar Four: Referrals and Events

Referral relationships, both patient-to-patient and physician-to-physician, remain one of the highest-converting channels in this category. Formalizing a referral program, staying visible at local medical and aesthetics events, and maintaining relationships with dermatologists, med spas, and primary care physicians in your area all compound over time in a way paid media doesn't.

## Pillar Five: AI Visibility

This is the pillar most practices haven't built yet, and it's the fastest-growing blind spot in plastic surgery marketing. When someone asks ChatGPT or Perplexity for a recommendation, those platforms aren't reading your website in real time. They're drawing on a combination of training data and, increasingly, live retrieval from the same reputation and directory signals covered in Pillar Two, weighted by how well-structured and independently verified that information is.

A practice with strong SEO and a good reputation can still be invisible to AI platforms if its structured data is incomplete, if its RealSelf and Vitals profiles are thin or outdated, or if it's never been tested against the specific procedure-level queries prospective patients are actually typing into these tools. This is also where brand-level and procedure-level visibility diverge. A practice can be well-cited for "plastic surgeon in Miami" while being completely absent for "revision rhinoplasty Miami," and that gap is where consults are quietly being lost.
