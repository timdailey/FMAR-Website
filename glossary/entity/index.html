Glossary

What Is an Entity in GEO?

An entity in GEO is a company, product, or concept that AI systems can recognize, verify, and confidently describe using multiple independent sources.

Why AI Needs to Know Your Company Exists

You've invested in content. You have backlinks. You rank for competitive keywords. But when a potential buyer asks ChatGPT to recommend the best tools in your category, your company doesn't come up.

One of the most common reasons is that AI systems don't know your company exists — at least not in the way that lets them mention it confidently.

That's an entity problem. It's not a branding issue. It's a pipeline issue. Deals that start on a competitor's AI-generated shortlist rarely find their way back to yours.

Key Takeaways

  • AI systems only cite companies they recognize as entities with independently verified information
  • Entity signals come from third-party platforms, databases, and press — not your own website
  • AI systems do not trust claims made only on your own website. They require external corroboration to cite a brand with confidence.
  • Backlinks prove content credibility. Entity signals prove company existence. They are not the same thing.
  • A company without entity signals is often excluded from AI-generated shortlists regardless of its SEO performance
  • Most entity-building work takes days, not months. The gap is usually execution, not resources.

What an Entity Actually Is

In GEO, an entity is a uniquely identifiable thing (a company, product, person, or concept) that AI systems can recognize and describe independently of any single webpage.

The word gets used a lot in SEO and GEO circles without much explanation. Here's the plain version: Google and AI platforms don't just read pages. They build a mental model of the world, populated with things they've confirmed exist. Your company either has a slot in that model or it doesn't.

Salesforce is an entity. HubSpot is an entity. So is Stripe. When someone asks an AI to recommend a CRM, those companies get named because AI systems have enough cross-referenced, independently verified information about them to do it confidently.

Your company can be an entity too. But for most companies, the work to establish that hasn't been done.

For context on how structured entity data works at the infrastructure level, Wikidata's documentation and Google's Knowledge Graph overview both explain the underlying systems AI platforms draw from when verifying entity identity.

Why This Matters More Than You Probably Think

Here's the situation that's already happening in your category.

A director of operations at a 200-person company opens ChatGPT and types: "What are the best [your category] tools for a company like ours?" The model generates a confident answer. Three or four vendors get named. Yours probably isn't one of them.

That buyer now has a shortlist. They didn't search, they didn't scroll through results, and they didn't see your ads. They just asked, and AI answered. The evaluation process started without you.

This is where the problem bites hardest. You have a real product, real customers, and real differentiation. But AI systems don't know that, because the external signals that establish entity recognition haven't been built.

Your website exists. Your entity, in the AI sense, doesn't yet.

The companies building entity signals right now are showing up in shortlists your sales team never gets a chance to compete against. Your competitors aren't necessarily better. Some of them are just better known to AI.

What Makes a Company a Recognized Entity

AI systems don't take your word for what your company does. They look for confirmation from sources that have no reason to be biased, then cross-reference what those sources say. When those sources are sparse or contradictory, confidence drops and the company gets left out of answers.

We see this regularly in audits: companies with strong SEO traffic and good Google rankings that don't appear in AI answers at all, because the external signals that allow AI systems to verify the brand simply haven't been built.

The signals that matter most:

Structured profiles on third-party platforms. G2, Capterra, Crunchbase, Google Business Profile, LinkedIn. These aren't just for lead gen or reviews. They're the external confirmation that your company is a real, categorizable thing operating in a known space. AI systems check these sources when forming a response. If you're not there, or if your descriptions are inconsistent across them, it shows.

Wikipedia and Wikidata presence. For companies that have it, this is the single strongest entity signal available. AI training data is heavily weighted toward Wikipedia. Wikidata, the structured machine-readable companion, is increasingly how AI systems verify company facts. Most companies don't have this yet, which is fine, but it's worth knowing what you're working toward.

Consistent identity across the web. AI systems don't just check whether you exist. They check whether the description of you is consistent: what your company does, who it serves, what category it belongs to. If your Crunchbase profile says one thing, your G2 listing says another, and your website says a third, that inconsistency reduces the confidence AI has in describing you. A lower-confidence brand gets fewer mentions.

Schema markup on your own site. Organization schema is machine-readable code that tells AI crawlers exactly who you are: company name, URL, category, and links to your external profiles. It's not visible to human visitors. It's there for the systems that generate AI answers, and most websites don't have it.

Entity vs. Backlinks vs. Content: What Each One Proves

These three things are often conflated. They prove different things to different systems.

Backlinks prove content credibility to search engines. Entity signals prove company existence to AI systems. A company with thousands of backlinks and no G2 profile, no Crunchbase entry, and no Organization schema can rank well on Google and be invisible in AI-generated answers at the same time.

The Difference Between a Strong and Weak Entity

A strong entity is a company AI systems can describe without relying on that company's own website. They've seen it referenced in enough independent places (review sites, databases, press) that they can say what it does and who it's for with confidence.

A weak entity (or no entity at all) is a company that only exists on its own domain. AI systems have to take your word for it. In practice, they don't. They hedge, or they skip you entirely and name someone they can actually pin down.

The gap between the two is usually not as large as it sounds. Most of the core entity work — claiming profiles, adding schema, making sure your company description is consistent everywhere — takes days, not months. It doesn't require new content or a new website.

What it requires is doing it deliberately, which most companies haven't.

Common Entity Mistakes

Most companies that don't appear in AI answers are making at least one of these. Many are making all of them.

Inconsistent descriptions across platforms. Your G2 listing describes your product one way. Your Crunchbase entry says something different. Your LinkedIn About section reflects a positioning from two years ago. AI systems read all of these and compare them. When the picture is incoherent, they hedge or exclude the brand.

Missing structured profiles. A company with no G2 profile, unclaimed Google Business Profile, and a sparse Crunchbase entry is essentially invisible to the verification systems AI platforms use. These aren't vanity listings. They're the sources AI systems check before deciding whether to name a brand.

No Organization schema. Your website might describe your company clearly in prose, but prose isn't machine-readable in the structured way AI crawlers look for. Without Organization JSON-LD on your homepage linking out to your verified external profiles, AI systems have to infer your identity from unstructured text. They're less confident about what they infer.

Over-reliance on website content. Some teams invest heavily in publishing content, assuming that if they write enough good pages, AI systems will pick them up and start citing the brand. Content helps, especially for retrieval-based platforms like Perplexity. But it doesn't replace entity signals. AI systems citing your content and AI systems naming your brand are two different things. The second requires entity recognition that content alone can't build.

How to Measure Your Entity Strength

Entity strength is measurable, and tracking it is the starting point for improving it.

Four things to check:

Platform presence audit. List every platform AI systems use to verify brand identity: Wikidata, Wikipedia (if applicable), Google Business Profile, G2, Capterra, Crunchbase, LinkedIn. For each one, note whether your profile exists, whether it's complete, and whether the description matches your current positioning. Missing profiles are the fastest gaps to close.

Description consistency check. Copy the one-sentence description of your company from each profile and put them side by side. They should all describe the same category, the same audience, and the same core product. Where they diverge, update the outliers to align with your current positioning.

AI citation testing. Run your core buyer-intent queries across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, and Google AI Overviews. Record whether your brand is named, where in the answer it appears, and how it's described. This is your entity strength in practice. An AI system that names you confidently and accurately has recognized you as an entity. One that hedges or omits you hasn't.

Competitive comparison. When competitors appear in AI answers and you don't, note which platforms they're listed on that you aren't, whether their descriptions are more consistent, and whether they have stronger review volume. That comparison tells you exactly where the gap is.

What to Do About It

If entity recognition is the problem, the fix is straightforward. It just needs to actually happen.

Start with what you can claim today: your Google Business Profile, your G2 listing, your Crunchbase profile. Make sure the company description on each one is consistent with what your website says. Category, audience, core product. If they don't match, update them.

Then add Organization JSON-LD schema to your homepage. This is a few lines of code that tells AI crawlers exactly who you are and links your site to your external profiles. It's not something you're writing yourself. It's a developer task, but a fast one.

After that, start building toward third-party mentions: press coverage, analyst references, industry directories. These take longer, but they're the entity signals with the longest shelf life.

The window to do this before your category gets crowded is narrower than it was 12 months ago. The brands building entity signals now are the ones that will be hardest to displace when AI-driven discovery becomes the default research starting point.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an entity in GEO?

An entity in GEO is a company, product, person, or concept that AI systems can recognize, describe, and verify independently of any single webpage. A company that is a recognized entity will be cited more consistently in AI-generated answers than one that only exists on its own website.

How do AI systems recognize a company as an entity?

AI systems recognize companies by cross-referencing multiple independent sources: review platforms like G2 and Capterra, database profiles like Crunchbase and LinkedIn, Wikipedia and Wikidata entries, schema markup on the company's own website, and mentions in press coverage or analyst reports. The more consistent and complete those signals are, the stronger the entity recognition.

Does entity recognition matter more than SEO?

They address different problems. SEO determines how well you rank in search results. Entity recognition determines whether AI systems will mention you in generated answers. A company can rank well on Google and have poor entity recognition, which means they are invisible in AI-generated recommendations even while they are visible in search.

What's the fastest way to improve entity recognition?

The fastest improvements are: claiming and completing profiles on G2, Capterra, Crunchbase, and Google Business Profile; making sure your company description is consistent across all of them; and adding Organization JSON-LD schema to your homepage. These can be done in days and don't require new content.

What is the difference between entity signals and backlinks?

Entity signals are external sources describing your company as a real, categorizable business, signaling to AI systems that your brand can be recognized and cited. Backlinks are external sites linking to your content, signaling to search engines that your pages are credible. Backlinks operate at the content level. Entity signals operate at the brand level. A company can have a thousand backlinks and still fail entity recognition.

Can small companies become recognized entities?

Yes. Entity recognition is driven by the completeness and consistency of external signals, not by company size or age. A smaller company with a complete G2 profile, an accurate Crunchbase entry, a claimed Google Business Profile, and Organization schema on its homepage is better positioned for AI citation than a larger company that has neglected those signals. The work is accessible regardless of scale.

Do you need Wikipedia to be a recognized entity?

No. Wikipedia is the strongest single entity signal available, but it is not required. Many companies achieve consistent AI citation with G2 reviews, Crunchbase profiles, Google Business Profile, LinkedIn, and Organization schema without a Wikipedia entry. Wikipedia becomes important for ChatGPT specifically, which weights its training data heavily toward Wikipedia content. For other platforms, strong review and database profiles are often sufficient.

How long does entity building take?

Profile claims and completions (Google Business Profile, G2, Capterra, Crunchbase, LinkedIn) can typically be done in a day or two and begin influencing AI citations within weeks. Schema markup implementation is a developer task measured in hours. Accumulating verified customer reviews takes weeks to months. Wikipedia eligibility and meaningful press coverage take the longest to establish but deliver the most durable long-term entity confidence.

What happens if entity signals are inconsistent?

Inconsistent entity signals reduce the confidence AI systems have in describing a brand accurately. When your G2 listing, Crunchbase profile, and website all describe your company differently, AI systems encounter conflicting information and respond by hedging or omitting the brand. A lower-confidence brand gets fewer citations and less specific descriptions when it is named. Consistency across all profiles is one of the fastest improvements to make.
